Author/Artist: Ray Harm

Title: Racoon (Pronyonlotor)

Type: Lithograph

Year: 1958, Gift of Mr. & Mrs. Charles Allen, 1974, 180.L.176

This print depicts a family of racoons nested on a tree. Ray Harm sketches his work before he prints them and he does so up close, and in the wild instead of just sketching from imagination or copy. This print and its orientation is very similar to the birds that Harm primarily works with in most of his pieces. Each raccoon is on a different side of the tree, and this orientation makes the viewer move their eyes across the paper to observe each raccoon. This print creates a very soft and calm feeling, for instance, the raccoons are a family living together, and exactly what Harm tries to capture in his prints, the nature and wildlife up close and personal and without leaving out any realism. This Ray Harm’s print, shows the subject interacting with one another, in fact, a lot of his works are very flat and shows the subjects only appearing individually on the paper as if they are not in the same place at the same time, and are only painted in spots that would seem ideally natural. The raccoons look aware of each other’s presence and this is the way Harm likes his paintings. This print would stand out to a viewer who might be interested in wildlife. —Christopher Spratling

Author/Artist: Robert Hamilton

Title: Natural History of British Fishes

Type: Book

Date/Publisher:1842, Edinbugh: W.H. Lizars

Source: Special Collections and Archives, 597 H219n

In order to truly appreciate the production and quality of Audubon’s The Birds of America, one must first reference the other possible outcomes. In the case of Audubon, he initially found himself struggling to find a printer who would produce Birds. Eventually, Audubon struck up a business agreement with famous British engraver William Home Lizars. Lizars had ample amounts of experience undertaking projects similar to Audubon’s. When Lizars first became enamored by Audubon’s Birds, he was in the midst of working on plates for P.J. Shelby’s Illustrations of British Ornithology. Lizars was familiar with the medium, but a printers’ strike and a series of business choices by both Audubon and Lizars halted any efforts to produce prints from this agreement. Years later, Lizars was responsible for printing Robert Hamilton’s Natural History of British Fishes. Hamilton provided a prolific amount of work on various marine life throughout his career. Whereas Audubon passion soared to new heights, Hamilton’s great contributions saw the ocean’s depths. British Fishes is a field guide collection with illustration prints accompanying all information about these marine life. All of the fish are drawn with their natural color schemes intact, and due to the limitations of size, the fish find themselves in much more natural poses instead of the contortions that are prevalent in works similar to Birds. —Christian Gentzel

Author/Artist: John James Audubon

Title: Le Petit Caporal

Type: Engraving with hand coloring

Year: 1829

Source: Gift of Gladys England, 1975, 180.N.110

Le Petit Caporal is quite different from many of the prints Audubon drew for The Birds of America. Le Petit Caporal was a small hawk found by Audubon in 1812, and was named after Napoleon Bonaparte, as this namesake was a nickname for Napoleon. Thus, Audubon, having found the specimen during Napoleon’s greatest time of power used the nickname for the bird. There is only one bird pictured in this piece because Audubon found only one specimen of this bird during his travels, which he first encountered whilst it was on a fence post, as depicted in the print, in a regal and dignified pose, which is different from his standard positioning of the other birds in Birds of America. It lacks a background, as Audubon was unsure of the life style and characteristics of this hawk. Not finding a nest or any other specimens of this bird, Audubon decided to pose the bird as he had seen while it was alive. Audubon presumed the bird he found was a rare and new species and gave the name that entitles this piece. In current ornithology terms, this bird is known by the common name of a Merlin (Falco columbarius). —Lance Hostetler

Author/Artist: John James Audubon

Title: Columbian Pouched Rat

Type: Lithograph

Year: 1846

Source: Special Collections and Archives, Gift of Bill Steiner

Serving as a companion to his prolific work The Birds of America, John James Audubon also covered a bulk of the mammals of the North American continent in The Quadrupeds of North America. Audubon first rose to prominence for his efforts in documenting as many North American avian creatures as possible. Years later when collecting his works for Quadrupeds, Audubon trekked across the American prairie one final time at fifty-eight years old. Unlike the art found in Birds, Quadrupeds is not the life-sized endeavor that Birds is famous for. This collection forgoes the sheer size of the animals to focus on the wide variety of wildlife found in North America. Much like Birds, Quadrupeds stages the subjects in a natural environment. The framing of the Columbian pouched rats in question are no different. The rodents are portrayed as a trio to offer multiple perspectives of the animal in question. The aerial view allows for the audience to see the size the rat’s pouches relative to the rest of its physique. Through this print we learn that Columbian pouched rats reside in burrows as their natural habit, and viewers can also see that the rats populate areas close to bodies of water due to the lake in the background. It is also displayed that the rats can survive in sparse lands as evident in the print’s lack of vegetation. —Christian Gentzel

Author/Artist: José De Acosta

Title: Historie naturael en morael van de Westersche Indien

Type: Book

Year: 1971

Source: Special Collections and Archives, 980 A185h

One of the most notable works created by José De Acosta would be The Natural and Moral History of the Indies. This work was published in 1590 by José De Acosta and served to recount his findings as the new world was being investigated. Acosta’s work served many purposes such as convincing the European people that there was in fact a whole new continent and people did indeed live there. The book also provided one of, if not the first detailed descriptions of things such as the uses of coco, the geography of Latin America, and Aztec history. Acosta went far beyond simply giving descriptions and observations as to what was going on in the new world. He sought to give cause as to why the things that were happening actually happened and how they might have come about. José De Acosta acknowledged that he was one of the first authors to try and give cause and reason to things that were happening in the world. Although Acosta died in 1600, his works helped shape the world’s understanding of this new continent that had been discovered and what might be lurking on it. This work belongs in our exhibit because it gives us insight into our continent before it was investigated while at the same time giving us reasoning and descriptions of the events that transpired at the time. —Bradley Atkinson
