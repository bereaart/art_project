Author/Artist: Rex Brasher

Title: Birds and Trees of North America

Type: Book

Date/Publisher: 1961, New York: Columbia University Press

Source: Special Collections and Archives, 598.2 B823b vol 1-4

The Birds and Trees of North America displays Brasher’s desire to convey birds in their natural habitat. Rex Brasher had a dream since the age of eight to discover and display birds in North America. Brasher achieved his goal by recording more than twice the amount of birds in North America than his predecessor James Audubon. Brasher had a vengeance towards Audubon because he would not meet with his dad when he took a journey just to see him. Once Brasher heard this story, he made it his life’s mission to record more birds than Audubon. During his journey throughout North America, Brasher recorded several species of birds that are now extinct such as the Heath Hen, Passenger Pigeon and Eskimo Curlew. These birds may have never been discovered if not for Brasher. After his journey, Brasher decided to have his drawings printed in black and white for cost efficiency. He received these prints and established a new technique by combining airbrushing and the use of stencils to color the prints. In this print of the flamingos, Brasher focused on the perfection of these birds such as making them as lifelike as possible and portraying the flamingos in their natural habitat. There is also rich color in this print with the bold pinks which came from the airbrush and stencil technique that Brasher developed for these prints. —Annelise Hackney

Author/Artist: John James Audubon

Title: Prothonotary Warbler

Type: Engraving and etching with applied watercolor

Year: 1827, Gift of Gladys England, 1975, 180.N.163

Captured in Audubon’s publication of Birds of America, the Prothonotary Warbler is presented with explicit detail and accuracy. The Prothonotary Warbler represents only one of the five colored prints produced by William Home Lizars. Lizars selected five drawings to demonstrate the different treatments of small and large birds in the printing process. Shortly after Lizars produced these five colored prints, members of his studio involved in the coloring process of the prints went on strike. As a result, Audubon turned to the Havell family to produce the colored prints for his book. Audubon presents two prothonotary warblers, each in a different position. The placement of the two warblers provides information of how the species look from different angles, providing visual clues of any marks, textures and forms that contribute to identification. The Prothonotary Warbler may be found in the floodplain forests and swampy regions from southern New Jersey to north-central Florida and may be found in the mainland extending to Texas. As depicted by Audubon, the prothonotary warbler displays high contrast between the lower and upper halves of the body. The distinct color palette of a yellow body in contrast with its blue to gray wings and olive colored back can lead to identification of this species of warbler alone. In comparison to the other birds that Audubon captures in his published works, the warbler is small in size. In celebration of Audubon who has contributed to the field of ornithology through his extensive documentation of birds, the Prothonotary Warbler presents a reminder of extensive dedication and research that Audubon conducted during his lifetime. —Taylor Styles

Author/Artist: Theodore Jasper

Title: Struders Popular Ornithology: the Birds of North America

Date/Publisher: 1878, Columbus, OH; Jacob H. Studer & Co

Source: Special Collections and Archives, 598.2 B834

Theodore Jasper displays the real life aspects and scenery surrounding the American Barn Owls, Lanier Falcon, and the Pygmy Burrowing Hawk in this print. Jasper spent his lifetime studying ornithology; the scientific study of birds. Jasper’s prints were similar because of his tendency to group different birds with no correlation together in one print. That is displayed in this print with the Owls, Falcon, and Hawk. All of Jasper’s prints were reduced to one quarter of their natural size, which affected the cost of the book. The important aspect of this print and all of the prints in this book was the affordability. Struders Popular Ornithology: the Birds of North America was the first bird identification book that was affordable for common people, unlike prints that came before such as James Audubon’s book of North American birds. Although not the best quality of prints, Struders Popular Ornithology: the Birds of North America was one of the most desired bird books of the nineteenth century based on its affordability. —Annelise Hackney

Author/Artist: John James Audubon

Title: Nighy Heron

Type: Hand-colored chromolithograph

Year: 1860

Source: Transfer from Hutchins Library, 1985, 180.L.263

The Night Heron is a print from one of Audubon’s most respected works: The Birds of America. Audubon provides basic facts about the specimen in his field guide accompaniment. The Night Heron is a southern bird known by many unofficial names in different states. A list of these names includes: “The Indian Pullet,” the “Gros-bec,” the “Indian Hen” and the “Qua-bird.” These birds migrate at night making “loud hoarse notes that resemble the syllable Qua.” The Night Heron is important for understanding the overall work of Audubon because it is an example of a plate from his most famous undertaking. This piece in particular also gave the ornithological community new information about the Night Heron through the field guide. He found that three molting periods are required for the plumage of the Night Heron to fully mature. The immature birds were being misclassified as different subspecies when they were actually the very same bird. There are many other cases from the field guide as well, where Audubon was a contributor to discovering the anatomy and behavior of the bird under observation. In fact, he is credited with discovering 25 new species and 12 subspecies during his travels. He was even elected as a member of the Royal Society of London for his ideas in scientific work. —Lucy Begley

Author/Artist: John James Audubon

Title: The Quadrupeds of North America

Type: Color photo-lithography

Year/Publisher: 1851-54, New York: Published by V.G. Audubon

Source: Special Collections and Archives, 599 A916q

Since the name of Audubon is so universally associated with birds, his paintings of terrestrial mammals of North America might be a surprise for many people. After the publication of The Birds of America that earned John James Audubon worldwide fame, the last ten years of Audubon’s life were spent on documenting four-legged mammals. Even though the proposed project was very difficult to accomplish, Audubon thought he would collect, identify, paint the portraits, and write the description of each of them. With such an enthusiasm, he immediately announced the future publication of The Viviparous Quadrupeds of North America. Soon, he received a letter from his friend John Bachman who offered his help saying “…are you not too fast in issuing your prospectus…? … The animals have never been carefully described, and you will find difficulties at every step. Books cannot aid you much. I have studied the subject more than you have… Say in what manner I can assist you.” As a result, John Bachman did a significant contribution in producing the Quadrupeds by determining the validity of species, writing all scientific descriptions, and contributing his personal observation on habits of the animal. On the other hand, Audubon with the help of his son John Woodhouse Audubon, painted and produced 150 drawings that were included in The Quadrupeds of North America. Audubon used several artists to reprint his paintings. For example, a skilled lithographer, J.T. Bowen who was from Philadelphia had a contract with Audubon to lithograph and color his paintings. Printing, on the other hand was done by other Philadelphian craftsmen. —Sher Sanginov
